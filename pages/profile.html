Synposis

The main purpose of this page is to serve as an extended profile to go with my resume - to establish some of my basic characteristics and provide background information.

To that end, this profile is composed of six sections. First, a personal introduction. Second, my life experiences. Third, extended details about my education and my academic interests. Fourth, my career aspirations and achievements. In the fifth section, I discuss the causes and organizations that I support. Finally, I discuss some of my hobbies and interests.

My hope is that this page will be useful to anyone who is considering working with me, to paint a picture for them of the kind of person that I am.

The table of contents at the top of the page can be used to skip to any particular section of interest.

Personal Introduction

My name is Simon Norris. I am finishing the 2nd year of a bachelor of science at Athabasca University in the School of Computing and Information Systems (CiS), majoring in CiS with a minor in applied mathematics.

I am curious but risk averse - and I approach new challenges both eagerly and carefully. I work thoroughly and independently, and I am always willing to learn new skills in order to find a better solution, or a better method for solving a problem.

I also consider myself an effective communicator, who enjoys sharing their ideas and creations with others.

My greatest strengths are logic, analyzing systems, and problem-solving. I will elaborate on logic and analyzing systems in the following two paragraphs and then discuss how I believe my affinity for logic and analyzing systems, together with a love for learning and a resultant aptitude for critical thinking, make me a natural problem-solver.

Logical thinking has always been my modus operandi. Though my goals were not always set on software and computer engineering, my interests have always aligned with a love of reasoning. I have a natural appreciation for arguments that can be either correct or incorrect. The idea of formal logic is what pulls me towards the study of mathematics and science in general. Whether it is a blessing or a curse, I have an undeniable desire for an ordered world. A world that is composed of truths can hence be understood in the correct (and tantalizingly, the incorrect) way.

I like to think I have an 'engineering brain,' - one that is made for analyzing systems. What I mean is that I tend to view the world around me as being made up of systems which are themselves composed of components. I find most concepts easier to understand, and consequently to deal with, when they can be thought of in this way and I naturally tend to relate non-mechanical things to a mechanical analog. Concepts, such as politics or human emotion, that do not fit well into such an analog tend to be outside of my comprehension and interest. To me, any system that can be broken down into logical fundamentals can be approached in a diagnostic manner; through this in-grained thinking and tendency towards module-based diagnosis, I find that I have a knack for both the design and analysis of actual systems.

I am a natural problem-solver. A bold statement that demands both a bold font-weight and an explanation. The affinities I described above for logic and analyzing systems are a part of that explanation, but they are only half of the equation. I have had a life-long passion for learning. This is something I truly mean; it has held me back in some regard as I am often not satisfied with a surface-level understanding and am never truly confident in my abilities until I have a thorough mastery of the concepts that underly them. What learning, as a general concept, truly fosters however is critical thinking. My own passion for learning new things, in conjuction with the more structured and problem-solving based learning I have done through my formal education, have given me a capacity for critical thinking which the 'engineering brain' readily taps into whenever I am faced with a logical system. I approach problems from all angles; I try to consider all possible constraints and variables, both explicit and implicit, and I am never truly satisfied with the depth of my solution.

My greatest weaknesses are human-oriented. I do not possess a great deal of tools for handling the emotional needs of others. I can sometimes come across as robotic and disinterested when discussing things I cannot make a solid analysis of. I am not socially inept, but I am uncomfortable in social situations. I am very much, and always have been, oriented towards 'things,' rather than 'people'.

Education and Academic Interests

I am currently pursuing my first undergraduate degree. A bachelor of science in computing and information systems (CiS), with a minor in applied mathematics. I see my minor in applied math as a way to transform my CiS degree into a computer science (CS) degree. The difference between the two being largely that a CiS degree requires less mathematical foundations, and has many more programming courses available as core electives than a CS degree does. My senior level electives are focused on core CS concepts: distributed computing, computer networks, computer security, algorithms, and databases. My senior level math electives also reflect the underlying mathematics of computer science: number theory, discrete mathematics, and mathematical modeling.

My desire to pursue the equivallent of a computer science degree where one is not explicitly offered is both because these are the concepts that interest me, and because I intend to pursure further education in computer science or in computer engineering. Some of the core CS concepts that I will not be studying at a senior level in my undergrad are: data science, machine learning, and artificial intelligence. I also know that I may need to find some way to provide a demonstratable understanding of advanced computing theory as there are no courses available to me that are focused explicitly on this important area of CS. (There is a graduate course offered through AU, Theory of Computation (COMP674) which I can perhaps find a way to take either during or after I complete my undergrad).

I also intend to pursue further education in computer science or in computer engineering. I find the interface between software and hardware to be the most fascinating and compelling part of computing.

My principle academic interests are: the numerical algorithms that enable efficient data structures, operating systems, and embedded and distributed computers.

I utilized the ACM/IEEE Computer Science model curriculum to aid in understanding the core concepts of, and deciding how best to choose courses that would reflect, a BSc in Computer Science.

Career Goals and Experiences

My primary career goal at the moment is to get a software engineering internship in order to begin my career, and I will continue to tailor my resume and profile to that end as well as to pursue whatever opportunities I can to develop the necessary experience to make that happen. My current plans are: to gain experience in the information technology field if I can find a part-time position using my completed course-work as credential. To apply for any and all undergraduate research assistant positions involving programming at Athabasca University as I enter my third year; and to gain credible experience developing and designing software by contributing to large open-source projects.

Looking further into the future, I would like to continue to pursue advanced education and publish my own research. My dream jobs would be to become a software engineer specializing in the development of operating systems, or a computer engineer, and to have a faculty position as a professor of computer science - researching important issues in the fields of operating systems, embedded and distributed computers, computer security, and/or data structures.

Hobbies and Interests

Growing up I spent far too much time playing video games; this has shaped a lot of my 'life experience' so far. In the last couple of years I have distanced myself from this past-time and begun to explore the interests that have always been there on the fringes. Chief among these are microelectronics, building drones, model rockets, and the game of chess.

Microelectronics and more specifically, microcontrollers, have always fascinated me. Some of my first memories of them are being amazed at the layout of circruitry as my dad, brother, and I would disassemble various electronics; as a child I likened the layouts to tiny cities, seeing the various interconnects on the board as roadways and each transistor and integrated circuit as so many office buildings, factories, and warehouses.

In recent years I have begun to develop this fascination in conjunction with my computer science education into my primary hobby. I have acrued a variety of soldering tools and microelectronic components and enjoy designing, wiring, and programming microcontroller circuits in my free time. Many of the projects I will fill my portfolio with are likely to contain the results of this past-time. It is an interest that I intend to develop futher both independently and by exploring embedded computing in my formal education.

Quad-copter drones are a newly developed interest of mine; I am not so much interested in operating them as I am in building and programming them. Last year I began to explore building my own quad-copter and programming my own flight control systems. This has developed into my first major microcontroller project. Much of my early interest in physics has been resurrected by the preliminary research for this project and autonomous flight has become a field that I am having a great deal of fun learning about and exploring. However, when I was a kid I would build and launch model rockets with my dad. As a young adult this is something I fell out of touch with, but I have begun building and launching them on my own again. I experience a great deal of joy in the simplicity and power of the concepts behind rocketry. This is the interest that I am perhaps most keen on sharing with others who enjoy it.

The game of chess is one that I think I used to flirt with only because of its romanticism. Ever since I began to focus a little more deeply on things however I have come to develop a much more fundamental appreciation for positional play. Looking at the game from this new angle has made it much more strategic and interesting. I no longer have a desire to play chess because it is regarded as the game of kings; now I am playing chess because I find it a deeply engaging and exciting game of strategy. I am currently playing on chess.com and studying recorded games on my board, still learning the more advanced fundamentals and mechanics. But, perhaps in the near future I will be able to update this section with an impressive rank and a thirst for skilled challengers.

Aside from these principle interests outlined above I also enjoy: reading non-fiction, especially about the history of mathematics and engineering, observing the night sky (though I took an introductory astronomy course once-upon-a-time I don't even own a telescope at the moment), and going camping with as little gear as possible and practicing general outdoorsmanship.
