Q: What would you say to inspire younger people?
A: "If you train yourself in fundamentals, and get exposed 
to interesting challenges, you almost can't help but produce 
great thing."

Said his strengths come from understanding the 
basics of physical sciences and continuous math. (physics background)

Q: What do you think about CS students using AI like 
Claude? How does it affect their learning?
A: "Well, thats the question of the day isn't it."
   2 perspectives.
    If you're new to programming, and don't have a strong grasp 
of the fundamentals, then you miss bugs and logic flaws that would 
be obvious to veteran programmers. In this case, it has a 
negative impact on new programmers who need to be 
developing mastery of the fundamentals.
    More experienced programmers who utilize a "spec > review > test" 
workflow can benefit from reviewing AI code. 
"I treat the prompt as a spec"
  Once you've learned the fundamentals, you can learn mastery by 
reviewing code. 
<br>
Math and algorithms are still key in CS knowledge backgrounds, and 
"backgrounds in continuous math are rare among CS students and grads and 
highly desireable in any specialization that involves simulation."
<br>
likened simulation to Alan Turing's paper "The Imitation Game"
 - you have to create a breathing, interactive simulation for it to be believable
 - enjoys working on simulations because for this reason they keep your challenges broad

When asked about the cost of generative program and if he thinks its 
prohibitive he noted he's not an expert on the topic but that he anticipates 
the costs will continue to decline.

Advice for students:
"Computer science is very inter-disciplinary." 
"Build technology in order to <b>enable</b> something"
ask yourself: "why are you interested in CS? What do you 
want to accomplish? What can you combine it with?"
"I think that kind of thinking is really healthy for the field."
