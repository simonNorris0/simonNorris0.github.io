An Afternoon at The Ballet. A classic ballet set to a magnificent orchestra transforms the Northern Jubilee Auditorium into the mystical Swan Lake. Enchanting performances from the swans and Oddette/Odile. In the end, we were served half of the original tragedy.

Alberta Ballet: Swan Lake

March 28, 2026 Matinee at the Northern Jubilee Auditorium in Edmonton, AB

This is a double-header of firsts for me: the first blog post I've ever written, and also the first professional ballet I've ever seen. Growing up, my sister was a ballerina and I would go and watch her shows; yet sadly, I must have been too young and perhaps still too malformed then to appreciate the strength, technique, and emotion behind the shows.

My afternoon at the ballet was an evocative experience; I found the performances of the principal ballerina Luna Sasaki, in the dual-roles of Oddette/Odile, and the synchronization of the swan maidens captivating. Choreographer Benjamin Pech gently modernized the relationship between male leads Benno and Siegfried and retained the elegance of the original story. The Edmonton Philharmonic Orchestra brilliantly and faithfully brought Tchaikovsky's original score, and the show, to life.

One needs not even the faintest understanding of ballet technique to appreciate the skills of Sasaki. It was simply enthralling to watch her masterful stillness while 'on pointe' and the grace of her movements, when she allowed them, created a powerful delicacy to her dance; it was often as if she was a perfectly mechanical ballerina from some music box being spun around by invisible hands. There was much fanfare during the 32 fouetté turns, and it was stunning to witness her dizzying speed in the turns while on pointe, but from a less discerning eye what I think was most magical about her performance - both during Odile’s 'grand pas de deux' and throughout the show - was how Sasaki used the dichotomy between perfect stillness and graceful, flowing, motions in tune with the orchestra. She was technically brilliant, I am almost certain, but she was also mesmerizingly musical. All thats left to say about Sasaki is that I hope to see her dance on pointe again!

Oh, how I long for more swans! We were graced by a troupe of 16 white swans and in their synchronicity the formations during acts 2 and 4 were dazzling. What was displayed by the principal dancers as independent technical expertise was echoed by the swan troupe as collective elegance and critical timing. Taken as a whole, each dancer was one unit of a flawless real-time system. Through some combination of critical timing and meticulous attention to one another's position they made the blank stage into a canvas.
I do not know enough of the source productions, or of ballet in general, to have much to say about Pech's choreography; I will say only that his adaptation of the original story struck me as surgical. It seems apparent to me that Pech sought to highlight the relationship between Benno and Siegfried. I am quite certain that Benno was made into the sorcerer whom cursed the swan maidens, which if true is an interesting development; I think on the one hand that it certainly makes his motivations in betraying Siegfried deeper. But, does it not also complicate the possibility of forgiveness? This leads me into the other major adaptation - only Oddette dies. Act 4 ends with Siegfried firing his emblematic crossbow at Benno, who appears to dodge the shot, with Oddette at the utlimate end of the arrow's flight. Hence, Pech has at least left room for the possibility of reconciliation between Benno and Siegfried. "Hey, sure I tried to kill you, but you're actually an evil sorcerer!" Perhaps more importantly, in Pech's rendition, they are the only two of the three left standing.

From their dark pit beneath the hazy stage that became Swan Lake, the Edmonton Philharmonic Orchestra was the clock that pulsed every movement of the show. They played Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky original score and the more experienced dancers allowed their motion to be controlled by the stream of emotion that came from below. It came with a fury at times, and it came placidly at times - but it came consistently paced and always at the right moments. What virtues then did this live, analog, orchestra bring that a digital sound production team could not? They put their own emotion into their instruments as the story progressed; emotion that superimposed on the dancers above. I do not believe you could get the same experience without these two human machines working in tandem.

I was graced by having my partner beside me at the show; and I could feel the emotion of the story amplified so much more by being able to hold her hand during. And also, I thought of my sister. I was here connecting with an art form that she has loved so dearly and for so long, her art form. I will certainly continue to visit the Alberta Ballet, and to seek out another production of Swan Lake - for my own enjoyment and personal growth, to learn more about the art, to share in more moving moments with my partner, and to connect with my family.
